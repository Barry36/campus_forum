Future opportunities and challenges

utah778/iStock/Getty Images

A social audit is evaluating and reporting on how a company makes progress towards their social responsibility priorities. There are no requirements to conduct a social audit, but some companies feel it’s their duty to do so.

To report on their progress, a company may consider Triple Bottom Line (TBL) reporting. TBL is a framework that can be used by organizations to voluntarily report their progress on social, economic, and environmental results for a reporting period (Karakowsky & Guriel, 2015, p. 409). "Bottom line" refers to net income (revenue less cost of goods sold and expenses), but the triple bottom line goes beyond discussing financial performance to consider the impact on social and environmental parameters as well. TBL aims to equally address the following:

  • People — Social issues
  • Planet — Environmental issues
  • Profit — Economic issues

TBL has been applauded for improving transparency of business operations and their impact on society.

However, some challenges of TBL reporting are that there are no widely agreed upon measurement standards (which would help compare TBL reporting across companies) and they’re too subjective as a result (and could be seen as self-serving).

Beyond TBL reporting, companies may want to get certification from an external body to show their commitment to being socially responsible. Consider B Lab, a non-profit organization that certifies for profit companies that are a force for good. Certification is granted to companies that meet rigorous standards of social and environmental performance, accountability, and transparency (B Lab, n.d.).

Stop and think:

When it comes to socially responsible reporting, what stakeholders would strongly advocate for companies to report on their social and environmental performance in addition to traditional financial reporting?

Consider the following:

  • Socially conscious investors — Want to see that the company and those they associate with have responsible business practices.
  • Environmentalists — Want to see that companies don’t have a damaging impact on the environment.
  • Customers — If they don’t believe a company takes care of those around them, they can take their business elsewhere.
